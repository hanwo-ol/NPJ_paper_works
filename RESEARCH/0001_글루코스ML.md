## Novelty of the Work
The authors present Glucose-ML, the largest curated collection of longitudinal diabetes datasets released between 2018 and 2025. It aggregates over 300,000 days of continuous glucose monitor (CGM) data and 38 million glucose samples from over 2,500 individuals. The study also includes a comparative analysis and establishes a naive baseline benchmark for short-term blood glucose prediction across all datasets. ## Importance of the Work
Access to large, high-quality data is a critical precursor for building transparent, reproducible, and fair AI algorithms in healthcare. While wearable technologies generate vast amounts of diabetes-relevant data, strict healthcare regulations and low interoperability restrict researchers' access to this information. This collection minimizes these barriers, accelerating AI innovations for diabetes screening, decision-support, and management. ## Identified Literature Gap
Much of the existing AI research in diabetes relies on outdated, small, or simulated datasets, such as the 2007 Pima Indian Diabetes Dataset or the UVAPadova simulator. Algorithms trained on simulated or homogeneous data often fail to generalize to real-world settings with diverse glycemic variability. Furthermore, a fragmented research community has created silos, limiting the algorithmic innovation possible with larger clinical trial datasets. ## Achievements of the New Method
The study quantitatively demonstrated that the choice of dataset significantly impacts an AI model's performance. By applying naive baseline predictors (zero-order hold and simple linear regression) across all 10 datasets, they proved that a single algorithm yields statistically different error rates when tested on datasets with varying glucose dynamics. Based on these findings, they established best-practice recommendations for dataset selection, model design, and evaluation. ## Study Limitations
The paper's analysis specifically evaluated longitudinal CGM glucose data, omitting other valuable multimodal data streams like insulin doses, sleep metrics, or meals present in the datasets. Additionally, the collection lacks representation of pediatric and adolescent populations, and some datasets underreport racial and ethnic demographics. The authors also acknowledge they may have unintentionally excluded some recent open datasets, such as D1NAMO and T1DEXIP.
